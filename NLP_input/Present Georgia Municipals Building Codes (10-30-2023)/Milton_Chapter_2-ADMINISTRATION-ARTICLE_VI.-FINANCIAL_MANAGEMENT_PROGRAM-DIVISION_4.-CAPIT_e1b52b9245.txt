DIVISION 4. - CAPITAL ASSET POLICY

Sec. 2-386. - Definitions.
The following words, terms and phrases, when used in this division, shall have the meanings ascribed to them in this section, except where the context clearly indicates a different meaning:
Asset acquisition means the various methods by which the city acquires assets. The term "asset acquisition" includes, but is not limited to, the following methods:
(1)
Purchase;
(2)
Donation;
(3)
Lease/purchase;
(4)
Trade-in;
(5)
Forfeiture;
(6)
Condemnation;
(7)
Internal/external construction;
(8)
Transfers from other governments; or
(9)
Any other method which transfers title of any property to the city.
Asset capitalization amount means the city will capitalize purchased assets at acquisition cost plus costs incurred in preparing the asset for use. The city will recognize acquisition costs based on individual unit prices.
Capital asset means any real or personal property acquired by the city which has an estimated useful life of three or more years with an acquisition value of $10,000.00 or more. The term "capital asset" includes:
(1)
Land;
(2)
Improvements to land;
(3)
Easements;
(4)
Buildings;
(5)
Building improvements;
(6)
Vehicles;
(7)
Machinery;
(8)
Equipment;
(9)
Works of art and historical treasures;
(10)
Infrastructure; and
(11)
All other tangible or intangible assets that are used in the operations of the government.
Generally accepted accounting principles (GAAP) reporting means all assets capitalized under this division shall be included in the financial statements issued by the city and in the annual external audit.
Gifts/donations means the city capitalizes all equipment acquired through donations at fair market value on the date of the transaction; provided such gift or donation meets the capitalization threshold of this division.
(1)
If the equipment is new and the donor can furnish an invoice, the invoice would determine the fair market value.
(2)
If the equipment is used or no information is available regarding the cost of new equipment on the date of acquisition, an appraisal will be conducted to establish the capitalization amount.
Leased assets means the city capitalizes assets acquired under capital lease; provided they meet the capitalization threshold and a buy-out option is included in the lease agreement.
(1)
The capital lease must meet accounting standards for capitalization purposes.
(2)
For capital leases, where the title of the asset will ultimately be transferred to the city, the asset will be capitalized at the net present value of future minimum lease payments.
(3)
The city does not capitalize assets it acquired under operating leases.
Property. Property is divided into several classes, including:
(1)
Real property. The term "real property" means land and whatever is attached to the land that cannot be readily removed, such as buildings and permanent improvements to the land. The term "real property" includes infrastructures.
(2)
Personal property. The term "personal property" means property that is movable and further classified as:
a.
Tangible. The term "tangible personal property" means property that is moveable, such as:
1.
Furniture;
2.
Machinery;
3.
Automobiles; or
4.
Works of art.
b.
Intangible. The term "intangible personal property" means the right of ownership in property such as:
1.
Bonds;
2.
Notes;
3.
Contracts;
4.
Computer software;
5.
Programs; and
6.
Proprietary assets that are created or purchased and owned by the city.
(Ord. No. 06-11-05, 11-21-2006)
Sec. 2-387. - Purpose.
This division is designed to provide a comprehensive description of the capital asset and property systems of the city. The primary purpose of the policies are to provide:
(1)
For consistent and uniform accounting of capital asset transactions throughout the city;
(2)
Guidelines for physical control and accountability of capital assets; and
(3)
Guidelines for disposal and depreciation of capital assets.
(Ord. No. 06-11-05, 11-21-2006)
Sec. 2-388. - Classification categories.
(a)
Land.
(1)
The city will capitalize all costs incurred to acquire land, without regard to a dollar threshold, and place it in use. The acquisition costs of land should include:
a.
The purchase price;
b.
Ancillary charges;
c.
The assumption of any liens or mortgages on the property; and
d.
Improvements made to the land that are permanent in nature.
(2)
Examples of ancillary charges to be included in the capitalization costs are:
a.
Legal and title fees;
b.
Closing costs;
c.
Appraisal and negotiation fees;
d.
Surveying fees;
e.
Site preparation fees;
f.
Demolition costs;
g.
Architect and accounting fees;
h.
Insurance premiums during construction phase; and
i.
Transportation charges.
(b)
Improvements other than buildings (IMP). The city classifies improvements to land with limited lives, such as fences, parking lots, and walkways, as land improvements. These improvements will be depreciated over their estimated useful lives.
(c)
Buildings (BLD).
(1)
If the city purchases a building, the capitalized cost should include:
a.
The purchase price; and
b.
Other incidental expenses incurred at the time of acquisition.
(2)
If the building is constructed, the capitalized cost should include material, labor, supervision, and overhead, or the contract price, including costs such as:
a.
Permits and licenses;
b.
Architectural and engineering fees;
c.
Insurance;
d.
Title costs; and
e.
Interest incurred on tax exempt debt.
(d)
Building improvements. The city capitalizes costs of improvements to a building if the following criteria are met:
(1)
The expenditures increase the service potential of the building; and
(2)
The total improvement costs, including the contract price and engineering, architectural, and attorney's fees, etc., meet the capitalization threshold of this division.
(3)
Items considered as improvements include:
a.
Ramps, fire escapes, truck doors or other appurtenances;
b.
Modifications to comply with fire, health, or safety codes; and
c.
Conversion of unusable to useable floor space and upgrade of the space.
(4)
Repairs to a building are not considered an improvement unless they extend the useful life of the structure or increase the value of the structure (i.e., a betterment). The city considers ordinary repairs as operating costs.
(e)
Construction in progress (CIP). Construction in progress includes the accumulation of ongoing project costs that increase the value or life of the asset. Upon project completion, the construction account in progress will be closed out and costs will be capitalized into the appropriate asset category (e.g., infrastructure, land improvement, building, etc.).
(f)
Moveable equipment (EQP). Expenditures for individual items or pieces of equipment meeting the capitalization threshold shall be capitalized consistent with the designations of personal property, both tangible and intangible, as outlined in section 2-386.
(g)
Infrastructure. Long-lived capital assets that normally are stationary in nature and normally can be preserved for a significantly greater number of years than most capital assets shall be capitalized based on the capitalization threshold requirement of this division. Examples of infrastructure include:
(1)
Roads;
(2)
Bridges;
(3)
Drainage systems; and
(4)
Sidewalks.
(Ord. No. 06-11-05, 11-21-2006)
Sec. 2-389. - Annual inventory.
(a)
An asset listing is sent annually to each department for verification of the existence of each department's capital assets.
(b)
The department should identify any assets that it has which are not included on the asset listing. Also, the department should identify any missing assets.
(c)
Designated staff will investigate any missing items with the assistance of the department. Significant, unaccounted for losses of assets will be brought to the city manager's attention for further action.
(d)
The city manager's office may make an annual physical inventory to verify the accuracy of inventory records.
(Ord. No. 06-11-05, 11-21-2006)
Sec. 2-390. - Transfer or disposal of assets; notice.
(a)
All capital assets are city property and may not be donated, discarded or transferred to another owner without direct authorization from the city manager's office. Departments should notify the city manager's office of all surplus assets ready for disposition.
(b)
The disposition of the capital assets the city purchased with federal, state, or other grant funds must follow federal guidelines. The guidelines of the specific grant, or general federal guidelines, will dictate the duration of time that the asset must remain city property. The guidelines also will indicate the disposition of the proceeds from the sale of the asset.
(c)
In accordance with O.C.G.A. § 36-37-6, all sales by the city of real property or personal property with an assigned value of equal to or more than $500.00 must be offered for purchase, either by sealed bids or auction, to the highest bidder.
(1)
Notice. Notice of the sale must be published once in the official city newspaper or in a newspaper of general circulation in the city. The legal notice must appear not less than 15 days nor more than 60 days prior to the date of the sale. The notice must contain a brief description of the property and a legal description for real property.
(2)
If the sale is by sealed bid, the notice shall also contain an invitation for proposals and shall state the:
a.
Conditions of the proposed sale;
b.
Address at which bids and other materials may be obtained; and
c.
Date, time, and place for the bid opening.
(3)
Bids. Bids shall be opened in public at the time and place stated in the legal notice. The bids shall be kept available for public inspection for 60 days.
(4)
The city may reject any and all bids or cancel a proposed sale.
(5)
If the sale is by auction, the notice shall also state the conditions of the sale and shall state the date, time, and place of the proposed sale. As a condition of the sale, a minimum amount may be established for the sale to occur.
(6)
The mayor and city council shall approve by resolution the sale of all real estate prior to any staff action.
(d)
Property with an estimated value of less than $500.00 may be sold without regard to the provisions contained in subsections (a) through (c) of this section. Such sales may be made in the open market without advertisement and without acceptance of bids. The city has the power to estimate the value of the property being sold.
(e)
As set forth in O.C.G.A. § 36-37-6(g), the city may sell and convey, by quitclaim deed, parcels of small or narrow strips of land, so shaped or so small as to be incapable of being used independently as zoned or under applicable subdivision or other development ordinances or land use plans, or as streets, whether owned in fee or used by easement, to abutting property owners where such sales and conveyances facilitate the enjoyment of the highest and best use of the abutting owner(s)' property. The city shall only exercise its authority to dispose of property under this section where either:
(1)
There exists only one owner of property abutting the city property to be disposed of; or
(2)
If there exists more than one owner of property abutting the city property to be disposed of, all such abutting owners agree in writing to the manner in which such city property shall be disposed.
The sale of city property to abutting property owners as authorized by this section is not required to be submitted to the process of auction or the solicitation of sealed bids so long as the sale complies with O.C.G.A. § 36-37-6(g), this section, and with any additional conditions that the council may impose by resolution.
(Ord. No. 06-11-05, 11-21-2006; Ord. No. 20-09-454 , § 1(Exh. A), 9-21-2020)
Sec. 2-391. - Depreciation.
(a)
Calculated. The city records depreciation for its capital assets monthly. Depreciation is calculated using the straight-line method with no estimated salvage values. For purchased capital assets, the city uses the invoice date as the in service date.
(b)
Estimated useful life. For purposes of depreciation, each asset is assigned an estimated useful life. Useful lives are based upon the category of assets. The life of the asset begins when it is put into service, which is recognized as the invoice date. The estimated lives shall be based upon those recommended by the Governmental Accounting Standards Board, the Government Finance Officers Association, and the city's experience.
(Ord. No. 06-11-05, 11-21-2006)
Sec. 2-392. - Responsibility for capital assets and property inventory and control.
(a)
It is the responsibility of the city manager's office to:
(1)
Establish and maintain an accurate accounting of capital assets; and
(2)
Design and implement continually improved operating procedures for activities required as a result of this division.
(b)
It shall be the responsibility of all city departments to cooperate in the capitalization process and to perform the annual inventory.
(Ord. No. 06-11-05, 11-21-2006)
