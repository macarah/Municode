ARTICLE I. - IN GENERAL

Sec. 10-1. - City designated bird sanctuary.
(a)
Designation of bird sanctuary. The entire area embraced within the corporate limits of the city is hereby designated as a bird sanctuary.
(b)
Shooting, hunting, molesting birds prohibited; exception. It shall be unlawful to trap, hunt, shoot or attempt to shoot, or molest in any manner any bird or wild fowl or to rob bird nests or wild fowl nests. Provided, however, if vandal birds are found to be congregating in such numbers in a particular locality that they constitute a nuisance or menace to health or property in the opinion of the proper health authorities of the city, such authority is authorized to approve the disposal of such birds.
(c)
Preventing vandal birds from destroying crops. Any individual may prevent any such vandal birds from destroying crops or fruit without authority of health officer.
(Prior Code, § 4-1)
Sec. 10-2. - Animal and fowl—Running at large prohibited.
It shall be unlawful for the owner, keeper or person having control of any wild or domestic animal or fowl to permit the same to run at large upon any street or public ground of the city or upon the property of any other person.
(Prior Code, § 4-2)
Sec. 10-3. - Same—Impounding required.
It shall be unlawful for the owner, keeper or person having control of any wild or domestic animal or fowl to permit the same to run at large upon any street or public ground of the city or upon the property of any other person.
(Prior Code, § 4-3)
Sec. 10-4. - Same—Redemption or sale.
In case said impounded animals or fowl shall not be redeemed within five days from the date of their seizure, the police shall cause the same to be advertised and sold as an estray, as provided by the laws of the state, if the owner is unknown. But if the owner is known and fails to redeem such animals or fowl within five days after notice of their impoundment, said owner shall be deemed guilty of misdemeanor and subject to the provisions of section 1-12.
(Prior Code, § 4-4)
Sec. 10-5. - Barns, stables, hog pens; location; consent of neighbors.
(a)
Prohibition. It shall be an offense to build any barn, stable or hog pen within 50 feet of any of the streets of the city, except as hereinafter provided.
(b)
Written consent of adjoining property owners. Any person desiring to build a barn, stable or hog pen within 50 feet of any of the streets of the city shall first secure from all the adjoining property owners and from those owning property on the opposite side of the street within 100 feet of the proposed structure, their written consent to the building of said structure, and shall file said written consent with the clerk at the time of securing a permit for such building. The clerk shall not issue any permit for any barn, stable or hog pen to be so located unless such consent shall be filed with him.
(c)
Filing of consent; removal for noncompliance. It shall be unlawful for any person to build a barn, stable or hog pen without getting the consent aforesaid and filing it with the clerk, and such barn, stable or hog pen constructed without compliance as stated above shall be removed within ten days by the owner after notice in writing by the city clerk, or by the police at the owner's expense, as a nuisance at the expiration of said notice.
(Prior Code, § 4-6)
Sec. 10-6. - Cleanliness of premises for animals, fowl.
All places and premises in the city where horses, mules, cattle, rabbits, dogs, pigs, hogs, chickens, or other animals or fowl are harbored or confined shall be kept clean, sanitary and free of noxious odors and shall be maintained in such a manner as will effectively prevent the propagation of flies in or about such premises. The maintaining or ownership of a place or premises in violation of this section shall be a misdemeanor, and in addition, such place or premises shall be deemed a nuisance and may be abated in accordance with law.
(Prior Code, § 4-7)
Sec. 10-7. - Dead animals.
No person shall deposit any carcass of any dead animal upon any ground, public or private, within the city.
(Prior Code, § 9-6)

Sec. 10-31. - Definitions.
The following words, terms and phrases, when used in this chapter, shall have the meanings ascribed to them in this section, except where the context clearly indicates a different meaning. The word "shall" is mandatory and the word "may" is permissive.
Abandonment means releasing or leaving an animal on any property, public or private, with the intention of deserting the animal.
Adequate food and water means food and water which is sufficient in amount and appropriate for the particular type of animal to prevent starvation, dehydration, or a significant risk to the animal's health from a lack of food or water.
(1)
Adequate food means palatable, non-contaminated and nutritionally adequate food that is appropriate for the species. Adequate food does not include garbage.
(2)
Adequate water means clean, fresh, potable water.
Adequate shelter means a structure having three sides, a top and bottom which is suitable for the animal's size to protect it from cold, wind and rain and other elements. During cold weather, sufficient bedding inside the shelter shall be present.
Animal means any living thing, not a plant, other than man (Excludes: rodents and insects).
Animal control officer means any person assigned to enforce the provisions of this chapter as designated by the chief of the Unadilla Police Department.
Animal shelter means any facility operated by the city or in conjunction with the county or its authorized agents for the purpose of impounding or caring for animals held under the authority of this chapter.
Governing authority means the city council in which the legislative powers of the City of Unadilla are vested.
Guard dog means any trained or untrained dog that is maintained for the purpose of safety or security and which is reasonably expected to attack persons or other animals independently or upon command.
Humane care of animals means but is not limited to the provision of adequate shelter and wholesome and adequate food and water consistent with the normal requirements and feeding habits of the breed.
Inhumane care means any act, omission or neglect which causes unjustifiable physical pain suffering or death to any living animal.
Inoculation against rabies means the injection subcutaneously or otherwise, of rabies vaccine approved by the state department of public health, and administered under the supervision of a licensed veterinarian and a certificate of vaccination or inoculation issued by the veterinarian.
Jurisdiction means for the enforcement of this chapter, the incorporated areas of the City of Unadilla.
Owner means any person partnership or corporation owning, possessing, keeping or harboring one or more animals. An animal shall be deemed to be harbored if it is fed or sheltered for seven or more consecutive days.
Public nuisance. an animal shall be deemed to be a public nuisance if the animal:
(1)
Is permitted to go upon the property of another person other than its owner or keeper without the express consent of such other property owner or occupant;
(2)
Attacks or acts so as to menace pedestrians or other persons using public ways;
(3)
Barks, whines or howls in an excessive, continuous, or untimely fashion.
(4)
Is repeatedly at large.
(5)
Trespasses on school grounds.
Records of appropriate authority means records of any county or municipal animal control agency, records of any county board or health, records of any federal, state or local courts, or records of an animal control officer provided for in this chapter.
Restraint means any animal which is on a leash or at heel or beside a competent person and obedient to that person's commands, or within a vehicle being driven or parked on the streets and roads of the City of Unadilla, or in a fenced area within the real property limits of the owner. Invisible fence systems are allowed if they are maintained in good working order and keep the animal contained. The primary means of restraint within the property may not be by means of a chain, cable, rope or other stakeout or tieout device.
Running at large means any animal which is off the property of its owner and not under the control of a competent person.
(Ord. No. 2006-10, 10-10-2006)

Sec. 10-61. - Dangerous dogs and potentially dangerous dogs.
(a)
Identification of dangerous dogs; notice. It shall be the duty of the dog control officer, and his designees, to identify dangerous dogs, and the owners of dangerous dogs, within the territorial boundaries of the city. Whenever the dog control officer classifies a dog as a dangerous dog or potentially dangerous dog, or reclassifies a potentially dangerous dog as a dangerous dog, notice shall be given to the dog's owner in writing by certified mail to the owner's last known address of such classification or reclassification. Such notice shall be deemed complete upon its mailing.
(b)
Notice requirements. The notice to the owner shall meet the following requirements:
(1)
The notice shall be in writing and mailed by certified mail to the owner's last known address;
(2)
The notice shall include a summary of the dog control officer's finding that formed the basis for the dog's classification as a dangerous or potentially dangerous dog;
(3)
The notice shall be dated and shall state that the owner, within 15 days after the date shown on the notice, has a right to request a hearing on the dog control officer's determination that the dog is a dangerous dog or a potentially dangerous dog before the mayor and council;
(4)
The notice shall state that if a hearing is not requested, the dog control officer's determination that the dog is a dangerous dog or a potentially dangerous dog will become effective for all purposes under this section on the day after the last day on which the owner has a right to request a hearing; and
(5)
The notice shall include a form to request a hearing before the mayor and council and shall provide specific instructions on mailing or delivering said form to the city.
(c)
Hearing; notice. When the mayor and council receives a request for a hearing it shall schedule such hearing within 30 days after receiving the request. The mayor and council shall notify the dog owner in writing by certified mail of the date, time and place of the hearing. At the hearing, the owner of the dog shall be given the opportunity to testify and present evidence and in addition thereto the mayor and council shall receive such other evidence and hear such other testimony as the mayor and council may find reasonably necessary to make a determination either to sustain, modify, or overrule the dog control officer's classification of the dog. Within ten days after the date of the hearing, the mayor and council shall notify the owner in writing by certified mail of its determination on the matter. If such determination is that the dog is a dangerous dog or a potentially dangerous dog, the notice shall specify it is effective on the third day following the date of receipt by the owner.
(d)
Certificate of registration. Subject to the requirements of this section, the dog control officer shall issue a certificate of registration to the owner of a dangerous dog or potentially dangerous dog if the owner presents to the dog control officer or if the dog control officer finds sufficient evidence of:
(1)
A proper enclosure to confine the dangerous dog or potentially dangerous dog;
(2)
The posting of the premises where the dangerous dog or potentially dangerous dog is located with a clearly visible sign warning that there is a dangerous dog on the property. The sign required to be posted shall conform substantially to the design provided by the state department of natural resources and shall meet the following guidelines:
a.
The sign shall be in the shape of a diamond, similar to a standard highway warning sign, composed of .08 gauge aluminum sheeting and measuring 12 inches by 12 inches.
b.
The circle shall measure 10¾ inches in diameter. The figure of the person shall measure five inches from the top of his figure to the bottom of his feet. The top of the dog's tail to the person's elbow shall measure 6¾ inches. The word "DANGER!" shall measure 1⅛ by six inches.
c.
The sign shall utilize two colors: Standard yellow and black. The background of the sign shall be in standard yellow. The circle, the figures of the person and dog, and the word "DANGER!" shall be in black.
d.
In addition to the registration fee and the fee required by subsection (d)(3) of this section, the owner of a dangerous dog or potentially dangerous dog shall pay the cost of the sign and the cost for a replacement sign if the original is lost, destroyed or damaged.
(3)
Payment to the city of a fee in an amount set by the mayor and council to defray the cost of administering and enforcing this section.
In addition, the owner of a dangerous dog shall present evidence to the dog control officer of:
(1)
A policy of insurance in the amount of at least $15,000.00 issued by an insurer authorized to transact business within the state insuring the owner of the dog against liability for any personal injuries inflicted by the dangerous dog; or
(2)
A surety bond in the amount of $15,000.00 or more issued by a surety company authorized to transact business in this state payable to any person or persons injured by the dangerous dog.
(e)
Notification when dog dies, is sold or donated. The owner of a dangerous dog or potentially dangerous dog shall notify the dog control officer within 24 hours if the dog is on the loose, is unconfined, has attacked a human, has died, or has been sold or donated. If the dog has been sold or donated, the owner shall also provide the name, address and telephone number of the new owner of the dog.
(Prior Code, § 4-27)
Sec. 10-62. - Possession of dangerous or potentially dangerous dog without certificate; keeping of dog in violation of conditions of certificate; confiscation and impoundment; destruction of animal.
(a)
Generally. It shall be unlawful for an owner to have or possess within the city a dangerous dog or potentially dangerous dog without a certificate of registration issued in accordance with section 10-61
(b)
Dangerous dogs outside enclosures. It shall be unlawful for an owner of a dangerous dog to permit the dog to be outside a proper enclosure unless the dog is muzzled and restrained by a substantial chain or leash and is under the physical restraint of a responsible person.
(c)
Potentially dangerous dogs outside enclosures.It shall be unlawful for an owner of a potentially dangerous dog to permit the dog to be outside a proper enclosure unless the dog is restrained by a substantial chain or leash and is under the restraint of a responsible person.
(d)
Impoundment. It shall be the duty of the dog control officer, and any person under his employ, to impound the dog if:
(1)
The owner does not secure a certificate of registration in accordance with section 10-61
(2)
The owner does not secure liability insurance or meet the bond requirement of section 10-61; and
(3)
The owner does not keep the dog within a proper enclosure or permits the dog to roam or run free.
(e)
Payment of impoundment costs; notice. Any dog impounded under the provisions of this section shall be returned to the owner upon the owner's compliance with the provisions of this article and upon payment of reasonable impoundment cost, as fixed by a schedule approved by the mayor and council, as from time to time amended. Notice of impoundment under this section shall be given the owner, in writing, by certified mail, at his last known address. The notice shall state that if the owner has not complied with the provisions of this article within 20 days from receipt of such notice the dog shall be destroyed in an expeditious and humane manner; provided, however, no dog shall be destroyed under this section unless it has been held a full 20 days, exclusive of the date of giving notice.
(Prior Code, § 4-28)

The keeping of any dog in violation of this division shall be an offense against the city, and punishable, upon citation to the owner, before the municipal court.
(b)
Violation of sections 10-61 and 10-62 shall be punished by the issuance of a state warrant pursuant to O.C.G.A. § 4-8-28.
